Materials
PLA
PLA (Polylactic Acid) is our most beginner-friendly filament, thanks to its low printing temperature and minimal warping.
It produces crisp detail and a smooth, matte finish, making it ideal for decorative pieces, prototypes, and hobby models.
Because PLA is derived from cornstarch, it’s biodegradable and emits a sweet, non-toxic aroma during printing.
It’s stiff but somewhat brittle, so it’s best reserved for display items or low-stress parts rather than functional hinges or load-bearing uses.
PLA doesn’t tolerate high heat—avoid outdoor use or any application above ~60°C without additional coatings.
PETG
PETG (Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol) strikes a great balance between ease of printing and mechanical strength.
It offers excellent layer adhesion and chemical resistance, making it a go-to for beverage containers, mechanical parts, and functional prototypes.
PETG’s slightly glossy surface brings out vibrant colors, and it’s less brittle than PLA—so your prints can flex rather than snap.
You may see a bit more stringing or oozing, but that can be dialed out with fine-tuned retraction settings.
Its tolerance to moisture makes it suitable for parts exposed to humidity, though you should still store it in a sealed container.
ABS
ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene) is prized for its toughness, heat resistance, and impact strength.
It’s the classic choice for functional parts, automotive components, and engineering prototypes that need to withstand higher temperatures.
ABS does tend to warp, so you’ll get the best results with a heated bed (≥ 100°C) and, ideally, an enclosed printer.
Its slightly flexible nature means ABS parts can endure stress better than PLA, and you can sand or acetone-vapor finish them for a glossy, professional look.
Note that ABS emits stronger fumes while printing, so good ventilation or an enclosure with filtration is strongly recommended.
Nylon
Nylon filament brings exceptional toughness, abrasion resistance, and dimensional stability to demanding applications.
It’s perfect for gears, bearings, living hinges, and any part that needs to flex repeatedly without cracking.
Nylon can be a bit tricky—it loves to absorb moisture from the air, so you should dry it before printing and store it in airtight bags with desiccant.
With a higher printing temperature (usually 250–270°C) and a well-calibrated first layer, you’ll get beautifully strong parts.
Its slightly silky sheen and natural flexibility make Nylon a top pick for tools, jigs, and mechanical assemblies that need to last.
